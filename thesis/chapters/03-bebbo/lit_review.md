# Literature review — Digital Apps for Public Health (Bebbo chapter)

Factual summaries of works cited in the introduction's "Experiments of Digital Apps for Public Health" section and in the Bebbo chapter's literature review. Each entry records the design and measurement detail needed to assess internal validity and the two flavours of external validity used in this thesis: **representativity** (Neyman 1934 — does the sample's joint covariate distribution match the target population?) and **ecological validity** (Schmuckler 2001 — setting, stimulus, and response).

No analysis or editorial. Facts only.

---

## Outhwaite (2023) — the nearest comparator

**Citation:** Outhwaite, L. A. (2023). App-based support for parental self-efficacy in the first 1,000 days: A randomized control trial. *Frontiers in Psychology*, 13, 998170. DOI: 10.3389/fpsyg.2022.998170.

**Design.** Pilot two-arm parallel-group RCT, 1:1 allocation, pre/post structure. 4-week intervention, no follow-up beyond intervention end. No blinding. Not an encouragement design.

**Sample size.** 88 enrolled; 86 randomised (43 per arm); 79 completed post-test (Treatment n=39, Active Control n=40). Retention 89.8%. The "N=79" figure refers to the analysed post-test sample.

**Recruitment.** "Opportunity sampling through social media advertisements." UK, October 2020 (COVID period). Only 9.8% of those expressing interest ultimately enrolled — a highly self-selected funnel. No population frame.

**Participants.** Parents/carers of children 0–6 months in the UK. Mean parent age 33.88 (SD 4.05). 98.8% female (85/86). 87.2% held an undergraduate or postgraduate degree. 84.9% White British. 93.0% co-resident with a partner. 66.3% first-time parents. All reported "confident or very confident with technology" and had pre-existing touchscreen access. Mean Edinburgh Postnatal Depression Scale at baseline 8.76.

**Intervention.** The treatment arm received free access to the commercial **Oliiki** app — 1,026 daily age-appropriate activities across eight domains, with developmental rationales, progress tracking, and age-tailored content. Participants were instructed to use ≥3 times/week for 4 weeks. **Two researcher-sent reminder emails were issued during the intervention period.**

**Control.** Four weekly emails containing three activity ideas each, drawn from the same Oliiki bank, but with no age-tailoring, no developmental explanations, no implementation guidance.

**Outcomes.**
- *Primary:* Parental self-efficacy via the Perceived Maternal Parental Self-Efficacy questionnaire (PMPSE) — 20-item self-report Likert scale, 4 subscales, α=0.91.
- *Process:* Implementation fidelity — single-item self-report of average weekly app/email usage at post-test only. **No objective app-log usage data.**
- *Baseline covariate:* Edinburgh Postnatal Depression Scale.

All measurements via online Qualtrics survey.

**Results.** Treatment > Active Control on PMPSE: F(1,78)=5.39, p=0.023, Cohen's d=0.43 (95% CI −0.02 to 0.87). Within-group pre-to-post d=0.79 (Treatment) vs. d=0.41 (Control). Positive dose–response between self-reported usage and post-test self-efficacy in Treatment (partial r=0.39, p=0.015); no dose–response in Control.

**Ecological validity audit.**
- *Setting:* User's own home, own device, entirely online. Natural.
- *Stimulus:* Real commercial app, but usage was researcher-prompted (reminder emails + explicit usage recommendation). Active control stimulus (weekly curated emails from researchers) does not correspond to any naturally occurring channel.
- *Response:* Self-reported attitudinal scale on a self-report platform. No observational or behavioural outcomes. No app-log data. No follow-up beyond intervention.

**Representativity.** Self-selected UK sample of highly educated, predominantly White British, predominantly female, tech-confident parents of newborns. Not a probability sample; not weighted to any target population. Selection effects are strong on interest in parenting research and on baseline tech confidence.

**Direct comparison with the Bebbo chapter.** Outhwaite is the only RCT in this literature with a closely-matched design (encouragement-style trial of a real parenting app, online recruitment, parental self-efficacy outcome). N=79 analysed, single country (UK), 4-week intervention, no objective usage data, researcher-prompted dosage, no follow-up. The Bebbo chapter's encouragement design recruits across two countries (Serbia, Bulgaria), with substantially larger N, app-log usage data, and a longer follow-up window — and finds null effects. The pattern of self-reported dosage relationships in Outhwaite, in the absence of objective use data, is not directly comparable to the analytics-validated engagement patterns reported in the Bebbo chapter.

## Hall & Bierman (2015)

**Citation:** Hall, C. M., & Bierman, K. L. (2015). Technology-assisted interventions for parents of young children: Emerging practices, current research, and future directions. *Early Childhood Research Quarterly*, 33, 21–32. DOI: 10.1016/j.ecresq.2015.05.003. PMC: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5074684/

**Paper type.** Narrative/systematic literature review of 48 studies. **Not a meta-analysis.** No pooled effect sizes are computed. Included designs span uncontrolled feasibility studies, quasi-experimental work, RCTs, single-case experimental designs, and qualitative studies.

**Inclusion criteria.** Parents of children 0–5; technology-assisted parent education or interventions; parent (not child-only) targets. Excludes radio/podcast, DVD, telephone calls, and video feedback.

**Scope.** Predominantly US/high-income studies. Intervention modalities: web-based platforms, online forums, mobile devices (texts, emails), and videoconferencing. Predates the smartphone-app era.

**Organisation of the 48 studies.** Table 1 — 17 feasibility/acceptability studies (largely non-experimental). Table 2 — 16 impact studies (mix of RCTs, quasi-experimental, single-case). Table 3 — 5 comparative studies (technology vs. an alternative format), mostly RCTs.

**RCT count.** Approximately **18 of the 48 studies are RCTs**. Notable ones (modality / country / N / direction):

| Study | Modality | Country | N | Effects |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Antonini et al. 2014 | Web + video coaching (blended) | USA | ~37 | Positive |
| Baggett 2010 / InfantNet | Web + telephone/email coaching (blended) | USA | ~116 | Positive; 84% completion |
| Carta et al. 2013 | Home visits + SMS (blended) | USA | 371 | Positive |
| Enebrink 2012 / COMET | Web + discussion board | Sweden | n.r. | Positive at 6 mo; 53% attrition |
| Evans 2012 / Text4baby | SMS (digital-only) | USA | 90 | Positive (self-report) |
| Kaplan 2014 | Web + email listserv | USA | n.r. | Positive |
| Na & Chia 2008 / KidzGrow | Web-based | n.r. | n.r. | Positive |
| O'Mahen 2014 | Web + mobile + chat (blended) | UK | n.r. | Positive, sustained at 6 mo |

| Marsac 2011 | Web vs. DVD | USA | n.r. | Positive for both |
| Sanders 2008 | TV + web/email vs. TV alone | Australia | 454 | Positive for enhanced |

**External validity of the RCT evidence (critical for the Bebbo positioning).**

- *Representativity:* All RCTs use researcher-recruited samples. Populations are clinically-referred / at-risk (child brain injury, autism, maltreatment risk, postpartum depression, FASD) or convenience cohorts in specific healthcare or home-visiting contexts. None draw from a general population through a naturally-occurring channel.
- *Ecological validity:* Every intervention is researcher-created and researcher-deployed. **No RCT in the review evaluates a commercially released app, a publicly listed platform, or anything discovered through an app store or natural distribution channel.** Dosage is typically researcher-prompted (assigned modules, scheduled coaching calls, structured SMS sequences). Outcomes are predominantly self-report — Hall & Bierman explicitly note "only a few RCTs include multiple or direct measures of parent and child outcomes." Sanders et al. 2008 (TV broadcast + web/email supplement) comes closest to naturalistic reach but remains researcher-orchestrated.

**Key quotes.**
- "Stronger effects in terms of both engaging parents and promoting positive outcomes for parents and children may emerge in blended intervention approaches that use technology along with personal contacts between intervention staff and parents… Other studies that relied on technology-assisted program delivery without personal contacts often struggled to keep parents engaged, experiencing high non-completion rates."
- "Although a growing number of studies use randomized designs to evaluate the impact of technology-assisted interventions, most rely on parent report to assess outcomes. Only a few randomized-controlled trials (RCTs) of technology-assisted interventions include multiple or direct measures of parent and child outcomes."
- "It is premature to draw strong conclusions about the utility of technology-assisted interventions, but important to encourage further rigorous study."
- "It is premature to draw conclusions regarding the relative efficacy of different delivery methods."

**Important correction to common framings.** The review does **not** supply a meta-analytic effect on digital-only vs. blended engagement/retention. The comparison is qualitative and narrative; only three studies in the review directly compare technology-delivered to face-to-face conditions, with mixed results and no consistent superiority for either modality. The "digital-only underperforms blended" pattern is an across-study observation, not a head-to-head RCT finding.

**Bottom line for the thesis.** RCTs exist in the review, but **none are externally valid evaluations of a real, publicly-deployed parenting app**. Every RCT is a researcher-instrumented trial of a bespoke intervention with researcher-recruited samples and researcher-prompted dosage. This is exactly the gap the Bebbo chapter addresses.

## DeWitt et al. (2022)

**Citation:** DeWitt, A., Kientz, J., & Liljenquist, K. (2022). Quality of mobile apps for child development support: Search in app stores and content analysis. *JMIR Pediatrics and Parenting*, 5(4), e38793. DOI: 10.2196/38793.

**Paper type.** Empirical **content analysis** of consumer apps — not a review of intervention efficacy, not a commentary. Systematic search of Apple App Store and Google Play (US, January 2022), 1,348 results screened, 149 apps coded.

**Inclusion criteria.** Apps supporting developmental milestone screening or health-behaviour tracking for children 0–5; English; free to download; involved child information tracking.

**Findings on what apps actually contain.**
- 15.4% of apps (23/149) had developmental milestone screening as a primary function.
- 36.2% (54/149) provided any personalised feedback.
- Of apps in the milestone category, only 4% (2/53) provided milestone-dependent activities for parents.
- 63.8% provided no personalised feedback mechanism.
- Cultural competence: 12.1% depicted people of colour; 0% included LGBTQ+ families, caregivers of different ages, or family members with disabilities.
- Only 8.7% cited CDC/WHO standards or peer-reviewed literature as content basis.
- 22.1% had usability concerns (crashes, heavy text without audio/video). 40.3% required Wi-Fi or paid cellular data.

**Evidence on app efficacy.** None. No outcomes data. The discussion section becomes prescriptive (proposing an evaluation framework for what apps "should" include), distinct in register from the empirical results.

## Black et al. (2017) [draft cites as 2018]

**Citation correction.** Draft cites "Black et al. 2018." The canonical paper is published online 4 October 2016, in print 7 January 2017. No Black-led ECD paper from 2018 matches the description. **Correct year: 2017.**

**Citation:** Black, M. M., Walker, S. P., Fernald, L. C. H., Andersen, C. T., DiGirolamo, A. M., Lu, C., McCoy, D. C., Fink, G., Shawar, Y. R., Shiffman, J., Devercelli, A. E., Wodon, Q. T., Vargas-Barón, E., & Grantham-McGregor, S., for the Lancet Early Childhood Development Series Steering Committee. (2017). Early childhood development coming of age: science through the life course. *The Lancet*, 389(10064), 77–90. DOI: 10.1016/S0140-6736(16)31389-7.

**Paper type.** First paper of a three-part *Lancet* "Advancing Early Childhood Development" series. Literature synthesis, not original empirical work.

**Scope.** Global, with concentrated analysis of LMICs. Age range conception to 5, emphasis on under-3.

**Headline claims.**
- ~250 million children (43%) under 5 in LMICs are at risk of not reaching developmental potential, primarily due to stunting and poverty.
- Prenatal–age 2 is a critical window; "nurturing care" attenuates the effects of early adversity.
- ECD service access is "inadequate and inequitable, especially for children younger than 3 years."
- Multisectoral, coordinated programming is required.
- Implementation has lagged the evidence base.

**Relevance to a parenting-app evaluation.** Motivational only. Brief mention of educational television; no discussion of app-based or digital interventions. Does not address ecological validity or sample representativity.

## Attanasio et al. (2022)

**Citation:** Attanasio, O., Baker-Henningham, H., Bernal, R., Meghir, C., Pineda, D., & Rubio-Codina, M. (2022). Early stimulation and nutrition: the impacts of a scalable intervention. *Journal of the European Economic Association*, 20(4), 1395–1432. DOI: 10.1093/jeea/jvac005.

**Paper type.** Original empirical cluster-RCT with ITT and IV analysis. **Not a framework paper.**

**Scope.** Colombia (Cundinamarca, Boyacá, Santander); rural and peri-urban communities <40,000 population; children under 12 months at enrolment. LMIC-specific.

**Design.** 87 municipalities randomised (46 treatment, 41 control); 1,460 children (702 treatment, 758 control) assessed at baseline and follow-up. Intervention lasted on average 10.4 months.

**Intervention.** Enhancement to Colombia's existing government parenting program (FAMI — *Familia, Mujer e Infancia*): structured early-stimulation curriculum, facilitator coaching, improved nutritional supplementation. Delivered through weekly group sessions, monthly home visits, and physical pedagogical materials. **Entirely in-person; no digital component.**

**Outcomes.** Bayley-III, Ages and Stages Questionnaire (ASQ:SE), anthropometric measures, maternal knowledge and self-efficacy scales.

**Results.**
- Cognitive development +0.16 SD (ITT); 0.3–0.4 SD on treatment-on-treated.
- Stunting (height-for-age < −1 SD): −5.8 to −6.8 percentage points.
- Heterogeneous effects: poorest households +0.24 SD vs. +0.04 SD for less-poor.
- Dose-response: +0.209 SD per 100 additional sessions.
- Cost: ~USD 322 per child per year.

**Relevance to a parenting-app evaluation.** No digital component. Motivational/conceptual: demonstrates scalable, low-cost enhancements to existing delivery platforms can produce developmental gains operating through parental investment and behaviour change. Does not speak to ecological validity of digital delivery or representativity of online-recruited samples.

## Britto et al. (2017)

**Citation:** Britto, P. R., Lye, S. J., Proulx, K., Yousafzai, A. K., Matthews, S. G., Vaivada, T., Perez-Escamilla, R., Rao, N., Ip, P., Fernald, L. C. H., MacMillan, H., Hanson, M., Wachs, T. D., Yao, H., Yoshikawa, H., Cerezo, A., Leckman, J. F., & Bhutta, Z. A., for the Early Childhood Development Interventions Review Group and the Lancet ECD Series Steering Committee. (2017). Nurturing care: promoting early childhood development. *The Lancet*, 389(10064), 91–102. DOI: 10.1016/S0140-6736(16)31390-3.

**Paper type.** Second paper of the same *Lancet* ECD series. Literature review, not original empirical work. Lead author based at UNICEF; reflects both scientific synthesis and policy argumentation.

**Scope.** Global, aligned with UN SDG frameworks. Encompasses LMIC and high-income contexts; urgency directed at LMICs.

**Core framework — "nurturing care."** Five components: good health, adequate nutrition, responsive caregiving, opportunities for early learning, safety and security.

**Key claims.**
- ECD interventions require coordinated work across five sectors: health, nutrition, education, child protection, social protection.
- Caregiving behaviours, parental attitudes, and caregiver knowledge identified as central modifiable mediators.
- Evidence base for ECD interventions is strong; coverage remains low, particularly in the poorest populations.

**Relevance to a parenting-app evaluation.** Does not discuss digital or app-based interventions. Conceptual/policy framework only — establishes nurturing care and its components (responsive caregiving, knowledge, attitudes) as legitimate proximal outcomes for ECD programs. Does not address ecological validity or sampling representativity.

## Gertler et al. (2014) [draft cites as 2015]

**Citation correction.** Draft cites "Gertler et al. 2015." Published in *Science* 30 May 2014. **Correct year: 2014.**

**Citation:** Gertler, P., Heckman, J., Pinto, R., Zanolini, A., Vermeerch, C., Walker, S., Chang, S. M., & Grantham-McGregor, S. (2014). Labor market returns to an early childhood stimulation intervention in Jamaica. *Science*, 344(6187), 998–1001. DOI: 10.1126/science.1251178.

**Design.** Original 1986–87 Jamaica Nutrition Study was an RCT with stratified randomisation (age, sex) and 4 arms. The 20-year follow-up (2007–08) is an observational prospective resurvey of the original randomised cohort — not a new randomised study; assignment determined by original randomisation.

**Sample.** 129 growth-stunted children aged 9–24 months in poor households in Kingston, enrolled 1986–87, plus a non-stunted comparison group of 84 nearby children. At 20-year follow-up: 105/129 stunted re-interviewed (81% retention), 65/84 non-stunted (77%). No selective attrition in the stunted sample.

**Intervention.** Community health workers delivered weekly one-hour home-based play sessions over 24 months. The four original arms were: (1) stimulation only (N=32), (2) nutrition only (N=32), (3) both (N=32), (4) neither (N=33). Analysis pooled arms 1 and 3 into "stimulation"; arm 2 was pooled with control (no long-term earnings effect of nutrition alone).

**Outcomes.** Self-reported average monthly earnings at age ~22, log-transformed, 2005 USD. Three employment subgroups: all jobs, full-time (≥20 days/month), non-temporary (≥8 months/year). Secondary: years of schooling.

**Results.** Treatment earnings 25% higher than control; β=0.22 (p=0.04) for full-time, β=0.39 (p=0.01) for non-temporary. Treatment was statistically indistinguishable from the non-stunted comparison on earnings. +0.6 years of schooling (p=0.08). Permutation tests and stepdown multiple-testing corrections applied.

**Ecological validity.**
- *Setting:* Participants' own homes; follow-up interviews in naturalistic settings. High.
- *Stimulus:* Real-world delivery by community health workers, comparable to routine home-visiting programmes. Moderate-to-high.
- *Response:* Self-reported earnings (recall-based, no payroll records). Moderate.

**Representativity.** Specific subpopulation — nutritionally deprived urban Jamaican toddlers in the 1980s. No national probability frame. External validity of effect magnitudes to other populations or eras is not established.

## Lukusa et al. (2018)

**Citation:** Lukusa, L. A., Ndze, V. N., Mbeye, N. M., & Wiysonge, C. S. (2018). A systematic review and meta-analysis of the effects of educating parents on the benefits and schedules of childhood vaccinations in low and middle-income countries. *Human Vaccines & Immunotherapeutics*, 14(8), 2058–2068. DOI: 10.1080/21645515.2018.1457931.

**Paper type.** Systematic review and meta-analysis of RCTs. **Not a Cochrane review** (uses Cochrane methodology — GRADE, RoB, RevMan — but is a separate publication). A related Cochrane review on face-to-face vaccination education exists (Kaufman et al., Cochrane Database 2018, CD010038).

**Included studies.** 6 RCTs, 4,248 participants. Country distribution: Nepal (1), India (1), Pakistan (4). **All South Asia.**

**Interventions covered.**
- *Facility-based (3 trials):* Health worker education at birth or first vaccination visit; 2–3 minute conversations during vaccination visits.
- *Community-based (3 trials):* Audiotape + leaflets (Pandey 2007); structured group discussions with community action planning (Andersson 2009); 5-minute home information sessions with graphic cards (Owais 2011).

**Comparison.** Standard vaccination practices.

**Outcome.** Completion of DTP3 (three doses diphtheria-tetanus-pertussis), via vaccination cards or maternal self-report.

**Results.** Pooled RR 1.36 (95% CI 1.14–1.62). Subgroup: community-based RR 1.61 (1.19–2.18); facility-based RR 1.24 (1.01–1.53). GRADE certainty: moderate (downgraded from high due to risk of bias in 3 trials).

**Ecological validity.** Community-based: participants' homes / community settings. Facility-based: routine clinic visits. Both moderate-to-high on setting and stimulus. Response moderate (cards more objective than self-report).

**Representativity.** South Asia only. No trials from sub-Saharan Africa, Latin America, or elsewhere. Recruitment through specific health facilities and communities, not national probability frames. Limits generalisability to other LMIC contexts including the European setting of the Bebbo trial.

## Cross-cutting observations

- **Outhwaite 2023 is the only RCT in this literature that is even close to a methodological analogue** to the Bebbo evaluation (encouragement-style trial of a real parenting app). It has N=79, single country (UK), 4-week intervention, no objective use data, researcher-prompted dosage (reminder emails), no follow-up. The Bebbo chapter's design is materially larger (several thousand participants across two countries), uses app-log usage data, and has a follow-up window.
- **No RCT anywhere in this literature evaluates a publicly-deployed parenting app encountered through its natural distribution channel.** This is the headline gap, and Bebbo's contribution. Hall & Bierman 2015's 18 RCTs are all of researcher-built interventions delivered to researcher-recruited (often clinical or at-risk) populations with researcher-prompted dosage.
- **Attanasio 2022 is an RCT** but in-person, government-platform-delivered, in rural Colombia. Not a digital-app evaluation. Belongs in the "scalable in-person evidence" framing, not the "gaps" framing.
- **Gertler 2014** is a 20-year observational follow-up of a 1980s home-visiting RCT. Not a digital-app evaluation. Useful for the "long-term benefits of effective ECD intervention" framing.
- **Lukusa 2018** is a meta-analysis of RCTs on a different intervention class (vaccination education in South Asia, N=4,248 across 6 trials). Tangential to parenting apps.
- **Hall & Bierman 2015** is a *narrative review* (not a meta-analysis). It contains ~18 RCTs but none are externally valid in the sense of evaluating a real, publicly-deployed app under natural conditions. The "digital-only underperforms blended" claim is a qualitative pattern observation, and the authors themselves state "it is premature to draw conclusions regarding the relative efficacy of different delivery methods."
- **DeWitt 2022** is a content analysis of consumer apps (149 apps in US app stores, Jan 2022); supplies no outcome evidence. Useful for "what apps look like in the wild" claims.
- **Black 2017** and **Britto 2017** are *Lancet* series framework papers — motivational only. No primary data; do not discuss digital interventions.

**Citation-year inconsistencies in the .bib:**
- `Black2018` — bib entry has `year = {2018}`. Actual publication: online 2016-10-04, print *Lancet* 2017-01-07. Either fix the bib year to 2017 or leave as-is (the bibkey doesn't have to match the rendered year).
- `Gertler2015` — bib entry has `year = {2015}`. Actual publication: *Science* 2014-05-30.

**How this maps to the Bebbo chapter's intro positioning:**
- The "thin evidence base" claim is correct, but the *texture* of the thinness is specific: it is not that there are few RCTs — it is that no RCT in the literature evaluates a real, publicly-deployed parenting app under naturalistic conditions.
- The Bebbo chapter's methodological contribution is therefore not "another RCT of a parenting app" but **the first RCT of a publicly-deployed parenting app evaluated under naturalistic adoption conditions, with analytics-validated engagement and a representativity-optimised cross-country sample.**
